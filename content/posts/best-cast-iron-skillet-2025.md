# Best Cast Iron Skillet 2025: Tested and Ranked

Cast iron skillets have been used for centuries because they work. They hold heat better than almost any other pan, develop a natural non-stick surface over time, go from stovetop to oven without issue, and last a lifetime. In 2025, you have more options than ever — from classic American brands to modern premium finishes. Here are the best cast iron skillets on the market.

## Why Cast Iron Is Still the Best Cooking Surface

In an era of non-stick coatings and high-tech cookware, cast iron remains a top choice for serious cooks. Here is why:

**Heat retention**: Cast iron holds heat exceptionally well. Once hot, it stays hot — ideal for searing steaks, frying chicken, and baking cornbread.

**Oven versatility**: A cast iron skillet can go into any oven, including under a broiler, at any temperature. Most non-stick pans cannot handle high oven temperatures.

**Durability**: A properly maintained cast iron skillet can last 100 years or more. It is nearly impossible to permanently damage one.

**Natural non-stick**: With regular use and proper seasoning, cast iron develops a slick, polymerized oil layer that rivals modern coatings — without the chemical concerns.

**Flavor development**: Cast iron builds up seasoning over time that adds depth to cooked foods.

## What to Consider When Buying

**Weight**: Traditional cast iron is heavy. A 12-inch skillet typically weighs 7 to 8 pounds. Some modern brands offer lighter options, but lighter usually means thinner walls and less heat retention.

**Smoothness**: Older cast iron (and some premium modern brands) has a smoother cooking surface. Budget cast iron often has a rough, pebbly texture that takes longer to season smoothly.

**Handle length and design**: A longer handle keeps your hand further from the heat. Some skillets include a helper handle (a second small handle opposite the main one) for lifting heavy pans.

**Pre-seasoning**: Most cast iron comes pre-seasoned from the factory. Premium brands often have a better initial seasoning that is ready to cook on out of the box.

**Size**: A 10-inch skillet is versatile for 1 to 2 people. A 12-inch is the most popular size for families. A 10.25-inch is a common size that works well for most tasks.

## Best Cast Iron Skillets of 2025

### 1. Lodge Cast Iron Skillet (Best Overall)

Lodge is the most trusted name in American cast iron. Made in South Pittsburg, Tennessee since 1896, Lodge skillets are the standard recommendation for good reason.

**Key specs:**
- Available in 6.5 to 15 inches
- Pre-seasoned with soy-based vegetable oil
- Rough texture that smooths with use
- Dual pour spouts
- Assist handle on larger sizes
- Made in the USA

Lodge's 10.25-inch and 12-inch skillets are the best value cast iron you can buy. The pre-seasoning is solid enough to cook on immediately. The rough texture is a common criticism, but it smooths out within a few months of regular use and proper seasoning maintenance.

For the price — often under $30 for the 10-inch — there is nothing close to matching it. This is the first cast iron most people should buy.

**Best for**: Everyone, especially those new to cast iron.

### 2. Stargazer 10.5-Inch Cast Iron Skillet

Stargazer is a premium American-made brand that has built a devoted following among cast iron enthusiasts. Their skillet addresses the main complaints about traditional cast iron.

**Key specs:**
- Machined smooth cooking surface
- Flared rim for easier pouring
- Longer handle with hang hole
- Heat ring on bottom for stability
- Pre-seasoned with flaxseed oil

The machined smooth surface is the biggest differentiator. Right out of the box, the Stargazer is closer to a non-stick pan in texture than a freshly-opened Lodge. Food releases more easily and it builds seasoning faster.

The flared rim is a thoughtful design detail that makes pouring grease or liquids much neater. The longer handle is also genuinely more comfortable to use.

At three to four times the price of Lodge, it is a meaningful investment. But for cooks who want the best performance from day one, it is worth it.

**Best for**: Cast iron enthusiasts who want a premium experience.

### 3. Field Company No.8 Cast Iron Skillet

Field Company makes some of the lightest cast iron skillets available without sacrificing cooking performance. Their No. 8 (10-inch) skillet is a favorite among cooks who find traditional cast iron too heavy.

**Key specs:**
- Significantly lighter than comparable Lodge pans (about 4 pounds)
- Machined smooth cooking surface
- Curved sidewalls for easier food movement
- Minimalist design
- Pre-seasoned

The thinner walls mean slightly less heat retention than heavier pans, but for everyday cooking — eggs, vegetables, sauteed proteins — the difference is minimal. The lighter weight makes it much more practical to pick up and maneuver with one hand.

**Best for**: Cooks who want cast iron performance without the weight.

### 4. Le Creuset Signature Skillet

Le Creuset's enameled cast iron skillet brings the brand's quality and aesthetic to skillet cooking. The interior is enameled black, which is different from traditional bare cast iron.

**Key specs:**
- Enameled cast iron (no seasoning required)
- Available in 9 to 11.75 inches
- Black satin enamel interior
- Oven safe to 500 degrees Fahrenheit
- Available in multiple exterior colors

The enameled surface does not require seasoning and is easy to clean. However, it does not develop the same non-stick properties as well-seasoned bare cast iron. It also cannot be used under a broiler at high heat (check specific model limits).

The enameled skillet is ideal for cooking acidic foods like tomato sauces that would strip seasoning from bare iron. It is a different tool with different strengths.

**Best for**: Those who want cast iron without the maintenance of bare cast iron.

### 5. Victoria Cast Iron Skillet

Victoria is a Colombian brand that offers Lodge-level quality at a similar price. Their skillets are slightly lighter than Lodge and have a smooth, pre-seasoned surface that beats Lodge's rough factory finish.

**Key specs:**
- Available in 8 to 15 inches

For cooks looking for a budget-friendly alternative to Lodge with a smoother cooking surface, Victoria is an excellent choice. The flaxseed oil seasoning gives it a slightly better factory finish.

**Best for**: Those who want a smoother surface at Lodge pricing.

## How to Season and Maintain Cast Iron

Seasoning is the process of baking oil into the surface of the iron to create a non-stick, rust-resistant coating. Here is how to do it:

1. Wash the pan with soap and warm water (this is the only time you should use soap on bare cast iron — just to remove factory residue)
2. Dry it completely — use a towel and then place it on the stove over low heat to evaporate all moisture
3. Apply a very thin layer of a high-smoke-point oil (flaxseed, grapeseed, or Crisco) to all surfaces
4. Wipe away the excess — the layer should be barely visible
5. Bake upside down in a 450 to 500 degree oven for one hour
6. Let it cool in the oven

Repeat 3 to 4 times for new pans. After that, regular cooking with fats will maintain the seasoning.

**Cleaning after each use:**
- Rinse with hot water while still warm
- Use a stiff brush or chain mail scrubber for stuck food
- Dry immediately and thoroughly
- Apply a tiny amount of oil and wipe dry before storing

## Common Myths About Cast Iron

**Myth: You can never use soap on cast iron.** A small amount of mild dish soap occasionally will not ruin your seasoning. Just do not soak it or scrub aggressively.

**Myth: Cast iron heats evenly.** Cast iron actually has poor thermal conductivity — it heats unevenly at first. Once hot, it holds that heat evenly. Always preheat your skillet for several minutes before cooking.

**Myth: Cast iron is hard to maintain.** If you cook with it regularly and dry it after washing, maintenance is minimal.

## Final Verdict

For most cooks, the **Lodge Cast Iron Skillet** is the obvious choice — exceptional performance at an unbeatable price. If you want a premium out-of-the-box experience, the **Stargazer** or **Field Company** are excellent upgrades. For those who want zero maintenance, the **Le Creuset enameled skillet** eliminates seasoning entirely.

Whatever you choose, a cast iron skillet will likely outlast you. Choose well and enjoy cooking on it for decades.
