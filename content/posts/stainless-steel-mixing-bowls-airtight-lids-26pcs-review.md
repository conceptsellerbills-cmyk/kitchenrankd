## Stainless Steel: Why It's the Right Material for Mixing Bowls

Mixing bowls come in four main materials: stainless steel, glass, ceramic, and plastic. Each has use cases, but stainless steel wins for everyday kitchen use for several reasons:

**Durability:** Stainless steel doesn't crack, chip, or shatter when dropped. A glass bowl dropped on a tile floor is gone; a stainless bowl bounces. In a busy kitchen, this matters.

**Reactivity:** Food-grade stainless steel (typically 18/8 or 18/10 — 18% chromium, 8–10% nickel) is non-reactive with acidic ingredients. Marinating citrus-based or vinegar-based dishes in plastic can cause flavor transfer; in uncoated aluminum, metallic off-flavors develop. Stainless steel is completely inert — whatever goes in tastes exactly as it should.

**Temperature handling:** Stainless steel conducts cold efficiently, making it ideal for whipping cream (chill the bowl first) and ice bath cooling. It also handles hot ingredients — transferring a just-cooked sauce directly into a stainless bowl is safe and won't warp or leach.

**Hygiene:** Non-porous stainless steel doesn't harbor bacteria or absorb odors from previous uses. A thorough wash and these bowls are fully clean with no residual flavors — unlike plastic, which scratches and stains over time.

**Dishwasher safe:** All stainless steel bowls in this set are dishwasher safe with no degradation over time — unlike plastic bowls that eventually cloud and warp, or ceramic bowls that can develop crazing in their glaze.

**Slicer attachment** produces thin, uniform slices — cucumber for salads, radish for garnish, and similar thin-slice applications where a mandolin would otherwise be required.

The grater-over-bowl setup is faster and cleaner than the traditional board-and-box-grater approach: less surface area covered in shredded cheese, easier cleanup, and the grated ingredient lands exactly where you need it.
